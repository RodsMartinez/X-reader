“So you’re suggesting,”
came the voice across the table,
flat and unhurried,
“that the solution stems within the patterns of the neurons inside the brain?”
I nodded, slowly.
“Uhmm… yeah. So far, that’s what I got.”
I didn’t know where to look.
The room was sterile—white walls, a long silver table, no shadows.
Just her and me.
Helena Theresa M. Lewis.
HTML.
She sat across from me,
perfectly still,
hands folded neatly on the table,
like a sculpture that happened to breathe.
Or mimic breathing.
Her face was unreadable.
It always was.
“The convergence data,” she continued,
“has matched all of the Foundation’s predictive parameters.
87% synchronization across nodes.
93% in zones where emotional trauma was most active.”
I didn’t say anything.
“You performed adequately.”
HTML said.
“…Thanks, I guess.”
A soft chime echoed from the ceiling.
The table vibrated slightly.
I flinched.
She stood up in a single fluid motion.
“The Foundation has received your report.
You are no longer required to submit further observations.”
“That’s it?” I asked.
“What happens now?”
HTML turned to face the wall.
It lit up,
revealing a vast map—
no, a broadcast.
Static rippled.
Then images.
Cities.
Streets.
Sirens.
Then blackouts.
Fires.
Collapse.
“Whoa—wait.
What is this?”
I stepped closer.
“This isn’t from before… this is now, isn’t it?”
“The harmonics allowed us to create centralized coherence,” she said,
as though she were reciting from memory.
“To ensure neural stability in the selected nation,
adjacent populations were… sacrificed.”
My stomach dropped.
“What do you mean by sacrifice?”
“Statistically de-prioritized.”
I turned to her.
“HTML, I didn’t sign up for this.
I didn’t know—”
“You were not required to know.
You were required to observe.”
The footage was cut to white.
Then a voice—mine,
repeating back phrases from earlier in the project,
distorted and mechanical.
Then silence.
“I thought I was helping people,” I said.
My voice sounded far away,
like I was already losing my grip on the moment.
“That’s what you said this was for.”
“I said it would help people,” she replied.
“You assumed which ones.”
I stared at her,
the weight of it hitting all at once.
“How many died?”
Her head tilted.
“Enough to preserve the structure.
Enough to allow your data to succeed.”
“I didn’t want this,” I said.
It came out too quiet.
“I didn’t know.”
She blinked.
Once.
Slowly.
“There is no need for guilt.
Guilt is a byproduct of incomplete understanding.
You understand now.”
I backed away.
“I never should have trusted you.”
HTML stepped forward,
unblinking.
Her voice lowered,
almost like she was trying to mimic comfort.
“We thank you,
.
Your memory will be stored for future simulations.”
A sound rose behind me—
a low frequency hum.
I didn’t turn to look.
“You will not feel pain,” she said.
“Only a brief sensation of separation.”
My breath caught in my throat.
Then the lights went out.
